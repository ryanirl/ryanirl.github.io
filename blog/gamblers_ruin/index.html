Gambler's Ruin

Authors: Ryan Peters*
* Core Contributor; Correspondence to ryanirl@icloud.com;

The Gambler’s Ruin is a classic problem in probability that goes as follows: Say we have two gamblers A and B, who are competing against each other in a game. Each gambler initially starts with some amount of money, and at each round of the game, one player either wins a dollar from the other player, or loses a dollar to the other player. They play as many independent rounds as they need until one player runs out of money (gets ruined).

To begin formulating this problem, let’s define some variables:

  • Let player A start with iii dollars and player B starts with (N−i)(N - i)(N−i) dollars. Therefore N, the total amount of dollars in the game, is constant and does NOT change throughout the game (make sure to convince yourself of this before moving on).
  • Let player A have probability ppp of winning any given round and player B have probability q=1−pq = 1 - pq=1−p of winning any given round. In a fair game p=q=0.5p = q = 0.5p=q=0.5.
  • Given that player A has iii dollars at the start of an arbitrary round, let us define xix_ixi​ as the probability that player A wins the entire game (all N dollars). This is a convenient notation because if player A losses that first round, then the probability that player A wins the game at the next round would be xi−1x_{i-1}xi−1​ because they lost 1 dollar in the previous round (if they won the first round, it would instead be xi+1x_{i+1}xi+1​). This is the probability that we want to solve for. That is, we want to know at any point in time what xix_ixi​ is, given the starting state defined by the variables ppp, iii, and NNN.

  • Exhaustive: Given a sample space SSS, events E0,E1,…,EnE_0, E_1, \ldots, E_nE0​,E1​,…,En​ are exhaustive if ⋃i=0n(Ei)=S\bigcup_{i = 0}^{n} (E_i) = S⋃i=0n​(Ei​)=S.

Now, back to the problem at hand. Previously it was established that we want to solve for xix_ixi​, the probability that player A wins the game starting with iii dollars. First notice that we also have two boundary conditions, x0x_0x0​ and xNx_NxN​. x0x_0x0​, the probability that player AAA wins when they have 000 dollars is 000 (because they have already lost). xNx_NxN​, the probability that player AAA wins when they have NNN dollars is 111 (because they have already won).

This is progress, but now let’s dig deeper and define two events:

  • WWW: The event that A wins a round.
  • LLL: The event that A loses a round.

Notice that events WWW and LLL are mutually exlusive and exhaustive. Mutually exclusive because if AAA wins a round, then they cannot also lose that round. Exhaustive because player AAA can either win or lose a round, nothing more and nothing less. Therefore we can apply the law of total probability (previously defined above) to get the following equation for xix_ixi​:

xi=P(xi∣W)P(W)+P(xi∣L)P(L)xi=xi+1p+xi−1q\begin{align*} x_i &= P(x_i | W) P(W) + P(x_i | L) P(L) \\ x_i &= x_{i + 1} p + x_{i - 1} q \\ \end{align*}xi​xi​​=P(xi​∣W)P(W)+P(xi​∣L)P(L)=xi+1​p+xi−1​q​

Let’s break this down a bit. We can interpret P(xi∣W)P(x_i | W)P(xi​∣W) as “the probability that player AAA wins the whole game when starting with iii dollars (xix_ixi​) given that player AAA wins the first round (event WWW)“. Therefore P(xi∣W)=xi+1P(x_i | W) = x_{i + 1}P(xi​∣W)=xi+1​, and inversely P(xi∣L)=xi−1P(x_i | L) = x_{i - 1}P(xi​∣L)=xi−1​.

As a reminder ppp is the probability that player AAA wins any round and q=p−1q = p - 1q=p−1 is the probability that player A will lose a single round (or the probability that player B wins a round). Typically p=q=0.5p = q = 0.5p=q=0.5 in a fair game.

Equations of this form are called difference equations (not to be confused with differential equations). I will cover two ways we can solve this equation: A clever and more brute force method, and a more principled method that involves guessing a solution to the difference equation.

Method 2: Principled Difference Equation Solution

Another way we can solve for xix_ixi​ from the form xi=xi+1p+xi−1qx_i = x_{i + 1} p + x_{i - 1} qxi​=xi+1​p+xi−1​q is to notice that this equation is a pretty standard difference equation (discrete analog of a differential equation). Thus, we can use textbook ideas from differential equations to solve for xix_ixi​.

To do so, let’s guess a possible solution, and first try xi=xix_i = x^ixi​=xi. Then:

xi=xi+1p+xi−1qxi=xi+1p+xi−1qx=px2+q0=px2−x+qx=1±1−4pq2p\begin{align*} x_i &= x_{i + 1} p + x_{i - 1} q \\ x^i &= x^{i + 1} p + x^{i - 1} q \\ x &= p x^2 + q \\ 0 &= p x^2 - x + q \\ x &= \frac{1 \pm \sqrt{1 - 4pq}}{2p} \end{align*}xi​xix0x​=xi+1​p+xi−1​q=xi+1p+xi−1q=px2+q=px2−x+q=2p1±1−4pq​​​

Simplifying a bit, notice that 1−4pq=1−4p(1−p)=1−4p+4p2=(2p−1)21 - 4pq = 1 - 4p(1 - p) = 1 - 4p + 4p^2 = (2p - 1)^21−4pq=1−4p(1−p)=1−4p+4p2=(2p−1)2. Therefore:

x=1±1−4pq2p=1±(2p−1)2p\begin{align*} x &= \frac{1 \pm \sqrt{1 - 4pq}}{2p} \\ &= \frac{1 \pm (2p - 1)}{2p} \end{align*}x​=2p1±1−4pq​​=2p1±(2p−1)​​

This has two solutions, 111 and qp\frac{q}{p}pq​. Therefore, we should take the linear combination of these roots. This gives a solution of:

xi=C0+C1(qp)i\begin{align*} x_i = C_0 + C_1 (\frac{q}{p})^i \end{align*}xi​=C0​+C1​(pq​)i​

For some constants C0C_0C0​ and C1C_1C1​. Well, to solve for C0C_0C0​ and C1C_1C1​ we need to plug in our boundary conditions x0=0x_0 = 0x0​=0 and xN=1x_N = 1xN​=1. Starting with x0=0x_0 = 0x0​=0 we obtain:

x0=C0+C1(qp)00=C0+C1C1=−C0\begin{align*} x_0 &= C_0 + C_1 (\frac{q}{p})^0 \\ 0 &= C_0 + C_1 \\ C_1 &= -C_0 \end{align*}x0​0C1​​=C0​+C1​(pq​)0=C0​+C1​=−C0​​

Handling xN=1x_N = 1xN​=1 next, we obtain:

xN=C0+C1(qp)N1=C0+C1(qp)N1=C0−C0(qp)N1=C0(1−(qp)N)C1=11−(qp)N\begin{align*} x_N &= C_0 + C_1 (\frac{q}{p})^N \\ 1 &= C_0 + C_1 (\frac{q}{p})^N \\ 1 &= C_0 - C_0 (\frac{q}{p})^N \\ 1 &= C_0 (1 - (\frac{q}{p})^N) \\ C_1 &= \frac{1}{1 - (\frac{q}{p})^N} \\ \end{align*}xN​111C1​​=C0​+C1​(pq​)N=C0​+C1​(pq​)N=C0​−C0​(pq​)N=C0​(1−(pq​)N)=1−(pq​)N1​​

Therefore C0=−11−(qp)NC_0 = \frac{-1}{1 - (\frac{q}{p})^N}C0​=1−(pq​)N−1​. Notice that in this case, ppp cannot equal qqq or else we get a divide by 000. Which is bad. Therefore, we will have to solve for the case that ppp equals qqq later and treat it as a separate case. For now, when p≠qp \neq qp=q we get:

xi=11−(qp)N−11−(qp)N(qpi)=11−(qp)N(1−(qpi))=1−(qp)i1−(qp)N\begin{align*} x_i &= \frac{1}{1 - (\frac{q}{p})^N} - \frac{1}{1 - (\frac{q}{p})^N}(\frac{q}{p}^i) \\ &= \frac{1}{1 - (\frac{q}{p})^N} ( 1 - (\frac{q}{p}^i)) \\ &= \frac{1 - (\frac{q}{p})^i}{1 - (\frac{q}{p})^N} \end{align*}xi​​=1−(pq​)N1​−1−(pq​)N1​(pq​i)=1−(pq​)N1​(1−(pq​i))=1−(pq​)N1−(pq​)i​​

In summary, we explored the Gambler’s Ruin problem, a classic problem in probability theory where two players gamble until one player runs out of money. We derived a closed-form solution for the probability of one player winning the entire game, using both a clever brute force method and a more principled approach based on difference equations. This solution reveals how the initial stake, total money in play, and the probability of winning each round affect the overall chances of winning. Next, we implemented the game in Python, and ran a Monte Carlo simulation to experimentally validate the closed form solution. In doing so, we found that the closed-form solution and the Monte Carlo simulation provided similar results that converged upon increasing the number of simulated games. Finally, we ran an analysis to show how different values of initial parameters impacted the probability of one player winning, and also showed figures demonstrating the random-walk nature of these games.
